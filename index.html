PhD. in Computer Science
The Ph.D. thesis in Computer Science, titled “Sarcasm and Implicitness in Abusive Language Detection: A Multilingual Perspective”, examines the implicit manifestations of abusive language, looking especially at the role of humour. Starting from the linguistic and computational analysis of the characteristics of the implicit abusive language against women, immigrants and cultural minorities in a multilingual scenario, we formulated the hypothesis that information about the presence of sarcasm could help to improve the detection of hateful messages, especially when they are camouflaged as sarcastic. Observing that sarcasm is a recurrent element in this kind of texts, and tends to affect the accuracy of the systems of recognition, we focused on the understanding of the role of ironic language in hateful messages. We examined the multilingual characteristics of irony and especially of sarcasm, validating, with experiments of classification, these traits as features. Finally, corroborated the importance of its role, we evaluated, looking also at the significance of the results, the benefits of ironic awareness in systems of hate speech detection.
Grade: Excellent Cum Laude
This thesis, developed in co-tutelle between Università degli Studi Di Torino and Universitat Politècnica de València, has obtained International Doctorate mention.

Università degli studi di Palermo

Bachelor degree in Communication for Media and Institutions
The thesis titled "Multi-word expressions: problems of synonymy and of automatic translation" examines how some Italian words are commonly used, and normally classified as nouns, actually are parts of particular locutions. The problem emerges in the automatic translation of these expressions, because the translators do not "interpret" the locution enterely. We tested different Machine Translation systems, noting how these words are interpreted as independent of the other terms, altering its whole meaning of the multi-word expressions.

Contributions to wider community

  • #DeactivHate Laboratory: is an initiative started with its first edition in 2021 to introduce secondary school students to artificial intelligence, teaching NLP basics for linguistic analysis of texts from social media and the creation of a simple classifier to detect hate speech online. The laboratory has the main objective to 1) show how technologies based on AI models work, and 2) raise awareness about the consequences of hate speech online, especially in school contexts affected by cyberbullying. This initiative brought research techniques among students of secondary schools, fostering their curiosity and showing how the user-generated content is used to train models. The laboratory had a relevant impact, especially in the north of Italy, and was required in both technical and humanistic high schools.
  • Perspectivism: is a new theoretical framework that changes the common workflow of creation and evaluation of NLP models. Indeed, the datasets should be released with disaggregated annotations and information about annotators that help the exploration of their perspectives in the analysed phenomenon; the models should be trained taking into account the subjectivities encoded in the dataset (the perspectives of annotators or of the authors of the messages); and they should be evaluated against test sets that include and reflect the diversity of perspectives mined in the data. In this context, the paradigm of a ground truth (i.e., obtained by majority voting) will be replaced by different truths. The importance of human label variation in the dataset was previously considered in other fields of AI (i.e., computer vision); however, it is still a novelty in NLP practices. Today, thanks to the various editions of the NLPerspectives workshop, the community is bigger, and new collaborations with the investigators of human label variation and learning with disagreement are established. Indeed, for the first time, the LeWiDi shared task was hosted during the 4th edition of the NLPerspectives workshop at EMNLP 2025.
